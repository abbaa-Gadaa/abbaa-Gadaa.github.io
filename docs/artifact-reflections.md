## Artifact 1: Story of AI

**Customization for the Audience:**
This artifact was designed for both technical and non-technical readers. Rather than presenting AI history as a chronological list of dates and papers, I structured it as a reverse timeline — starting from tools people already use (ChatGPT, Copilot) and working backward to Turing's 1950 question. This makes the content immediately relatable before introducing unfamiliar history.

**Lessons Learned:**
The biggest lesson was restraint. My first version tried to cover every milestone in AI history. Through revision, I learned that a focused narrative with four clear chapters communicates more effectively than a comprehensive timeline. Cutting content made the remaining content stronger.

**Feedback Received and Revisions Applied:**
Initial feedback noted the artifact was "too text-heavy" and "reads like a document, not a portfolio piece." I restructured the entire page from dense paragraphs into a scroll-driven visual layout with editorial illustrations, two-column text-and-image sections, and generous whitespace. A second round of feedback flagged that the design relied too heavily on emoji-based icons, which appeared AI-generated. I replaced all of those with custom illustrations that maintain a consistent warm editorial style across the portfolio.

**Engagement:**
Building this artifact deepened my understanding of how AI's history directly informs current engineering decisions — the shift from hand-coded rules to learned representations in 2012 is the same architectural decision ML engineers face today when choosing between rule-based systems and neural approaches.

## Artifact 2: JobPrep AI

**Customization for the Audience:**
This artifact targets two audiences: recruiters evaluating my prompt engineering skills, and job seekers who might actually use the tool. The page structure reflects this — it demonstrates the tool's value with a real conversation example, explains the engineering behind it, and provides a live link so visitors can try it themselves.

**Lessons Learned:**
Prompt engineering is deceptively difficult. Early versions of the GPT produced generic advice regardless of the role. I learned that the prompt architecture — how you structure instructions, constraints, and output format — matters more than the length of the prompt. Iterative testing across different job roles (embedded systems, data science, product management) was essential.

**Feedback Received and Revisions Applied:**
Feedback pointed out that I was "describing, not demonstrating" — explaining what the tool does without showing it. I added a live link to the actual GPT, included a real conversation demo on the page, and added an input-output illustration showing the transformation from resume to interview prep. The second design revision removed decorative emoji cards in favor of editorial layouts with real images.

**Engagement:**
This artifact taught me the full cycle of building an AI product: identifying a real problem, designing a solution, engineering the prompt, testing with real users, and presenting the result. It's the artifact I'm most likely to reference in actual job interviews.

## Artifact 3: Neural Networks Made Simple

**Customization for the Audience:**
This is explicitly designed for non-technical readers. Every concept is explained through visual analogy rather than math — the cat-to-label classification example, the brain-to-network comparison, the Face ID use case. The goal is that someone with no ML background can understand neural networks after one scroll.

**Lessons Learned:**
Explaining something simply requires understanding it deeply. I couldn't use visual analogies effectively until I could articulate exactly what each layer in a neural network does without resorting to jargon. The process of simplification forced me to confront gaps in my own understanding.

**Feedback Received and Revisions Applied:**
The original version used CSS-only abstract diagrams (circles and lines) to represent the network architecture. Feedback noted this looked "AI-generated" and didn't convey information effectively. I replaced these with custom illustrations — a proper neural network diagram with warm-colored nodes and a cat-classification example that tells a story. The page structure shifted from grid-based card layouts to two-column editorial sections with images.

**Engagement:**
This artifact connects directly to stakeholder communication — a skill I use constantly. Being able to explain neural networks clearly to non-technical collaborators is as important as being able to build them. This page is evidence of that communication ability.

## Artifact 4: The Data Problem

**Customization for the Audience:**
This artifact targets ML practitioners and hiring managers who understand that data quality is the real bottleneck in production ML. The model-centric vs. data-centric comparison table is designed to signal practical experience — this is the kind of thinking that separates research prototypes from production systems.

**Lessons Learned:**
The research (Santos et al., 2023; Krawczyk, 2016) formalized patterns I'd seen in practice — imbalanced datasets, class overlap, and concept drift are problems every ML engineer encounters but rarely names explicitly. Building this artifact gave me a framework for discussing data quality that I now apply to my own projects.

**Feedback Received and Revisions Applied:**
This artifact was built after incorporating all prior feedback. It was designed from the start with editorial layouts, custom illustrations, and concise text — avoiding the issues flagged in earlier artifacts. The comparison table format was inspired by professor-approved portfolios that used structured data comparisons to demonstrate analytical thinking.

**Engagement:**
Of all four artifacts, this one most directly reflects my professional direction. AI infrastructure is fundamentally a data problem — pipelines, quality checks, versioning, drift detection. This artifact demonstrates that I understand the unglamorous but critical work that makes ML systems reliable.
